Internship Review

What were your expectations about your internship before you joined?

Before commencing my internship, I expected the role to be heavily focused on rigorous, hands-on coding, producing a large quantity of raw lines of code. I anticipated spending much of my time developing ETL pipelines in python, writing scripts to process and transform data and working directly with SQL queries to access data from the first day of the internship. I also expected a relatively fast-paced development environment, akin to startup companies or compact development teams. Additionally, I assumed the role would cover more aspects of data analysis as opposed to purely engineering data pipelines for downstream analysis.

What was the reality? How was it different from your expectations?

Several aspects of the internship differed significantly from the expectations I had been expecting. While technical development remained a core part of the role, projects, development cycles and pushing code happened at a rate slower than expectations. This was largely due to bureaucracy, security and data sensitivity within the organisation, with there being a large focus on project management, end-to-end task ownership, intense requirements gathering, documentation and iterative meetings during development processes reducing time spent coding. Additionally, much of my first 5-6 weeks at the internship was spent gaining access to systems and exposure to the complex Westpac data architecture and developing the technical required to meet development demands. This meant that I was only exposed to production level pipelines when I had a complete grasp of these concepts and their place within Westpac, ensuring I was prepared to contribute safely and efficiently to compliance heavy systems.

What lessons were the most important from your internship. Why were they important?

From the internship, aside from the array of technical skills I acquired, I learned that forming relationships and building connections within the industry can often be more important than possessing advanced technical skills within engineering roles. These connections are crucial to developing a strong network within industry that can be leveraged at a later stage for several purposes. Additionally, while technical competence is essential, I learned that the ability to communicate with team members and convey concepts is often required to sell ideas, finalise projects and ensure your work that is done is received and acknowledged vertically within organisations at scale.

After your workplace experience, what would you say your value proposition would be to an employer? How can you demonstrate this?

My value lies in my exposure to enterprise scala data engineering environments, my understanding of technical systems and how to deliver functional products under tight deadlines while accommodating organisational requirements. This includes my hands-on experience with cutting edge cloud orchestrated data pipelines in the Azure Synapse environment, knowledge of Python, SQL and backend frameworks, enterprise agile methodologies and familiarity within the Atlassian Ecosystem, demonstrated through experience and personal projects.

How did your internship influence the type of role in which you are interested?

Prior to the internship, my lens of software engineering was heavily geared towards full stack web development or backend engineering as the two pathways available to me post-graduation. However, since working so closely within a multi-disciplinary team of engineers, my perspective of software engineering has shifted to include roles such as data engineering, cloud infrastructure specialists, Dev-Ops, System Design Architects and Quality Assurance roles, all of which are critical to developing a functional software product.
